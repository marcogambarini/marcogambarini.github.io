Diffraction and radiation by a floating body

When simulating the effects of floating bodies on waves in a linear setting using boundary element methods (BEM), it is customary to represent the resulting wave elevation as the sum of incident, diffracted and radiated waves. This was difficult for me to visualize: I have put together some animations to hopefully make it clearer.
The decomposition of wave elevation is $$ \eta = \eta_i + \eta_d + \sum_i^{N_\text{dof}} \eta_{r, i} \xi_i$$ where \(\eta_i\) are the incident waves, \(\eta_d\) the diffracted waves, \(\eta_{r,i}\) the radiated waves for unit amplitude motion in the direction of degree of freedom \(i\) and \(\xi_i\) the amplitude of the body oscillations in the \(i\)-th degree of freedom.
We will consider the case of an ideally infinitely long rectangle with its long edge parallel to wavefronts (perpendicular to wavevectors), its short edge much smaller than the wavelength and its thickness small compared to both other dimensions. Such a body, as we shall see, behaves as a wave follower, that is it rides the waves while not perturbing them much; this is hard to see from the individual diffraction and radiation results.

Let's start with incident waves.

Let us now see the effect of diffraction: we solve the problem with the body held fixed.

The blue line indicates incident waves, the orange one diffracted waves, and the solid red line is the resulting field. We can see that waves are partially reflected, forming almost standing waves in front of the body, while behind the body the resulting waves travel with smaller amplitude and a different phase with respect to the incident waves. This would indicate that the body is able to attenuate incident waves very effectively. Actually, this would be true if the body was actually fixed, but we still have to sum the radiation contribution.

This is calculated by imposing unit oscillations to the body. Each time the body moves down, it displaces water, generating a pair of outgoing waves. These waves happen to almost cancel diffracted waves: here is the result.
